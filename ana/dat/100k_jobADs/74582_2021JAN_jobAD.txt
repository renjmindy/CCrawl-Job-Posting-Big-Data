 Two ways I flubbed my first job interview

I was very excited, prepared a job talk, and hopped on the train for my campus visit. 
 When the train arrived it was easy to figure out who, on the platform, was my host: the one dressed like an ecology professor, advancing towards me. So I offered a handshake and told him my name. There was a brief pause (which I thought nothing of at the time) and then he told me his, welcomed me, and took for a tour of the town on the way to my hotel. Let‚Äôs call my host ‚ÄúDr. Smith‚Äù.

I sat in the front row while the audience arrived, and while Dr. Smith stood up to give my introduction. He began this way*:

 ‚ÄúHuh,‚Äù  I thought,  ‚ÄúI wonder who he‚Äôs talking about? Laura, maybe, or Peter?‚Äù  But he went on about this student for some time, talking about going to lunch with him (use of the  gendered pronoun  at some point ruled out some of my guesses) and being told all about his research. As Dr. Smith spoke, a feeling of exquisite horror gradually crept over me. I know you‚Äôre way ahead of me here: he was talking about me. But I had no memory whatsoever of meeting him (or even taking him to lunch), and I had blithely introduced myself to him at the train station as one does to a stranger.  The other reason I didn‚Äôt get the job ‚Äì and the far more important one ‚Äì is that I had no idea what an academic job interview  was . I‚Äôd faced many such tests in the past (my comprehensive exam, for example), and I‚Äôd been good at them. I was, therefore, totally unprepared to suck at what the job interview  actually  is: a conversation in which the hiring department feels you out as a peer and colleague. I wanted to convince them that my research used the correct statistical tests; but they wanted to know how I‚Äôd contribute to their students‚Äô supervisory committees. I wanted to demonstrate my track record of planning interesting research; but they wanted to know how my future plans would complement theirs. I wanted to show them I‚Äôd read all the right papers; but they wanted to know what papers we could write together. In short: I wanted to show them I was an excellent grad student; but they wanted to know if I was prepared to be a good professor. I now understand that I was not.
